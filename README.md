## Overview

The Sedona Vortexes are a cluster of sites in and around Sedona, Arizona, that New Age practitioners believe to be centers of concentrated earth energy — swirling vortices of electromagnetic or spiritual force that can amplify meditation, accelerate healing, and facilitate personal transformation. The four most publicized vortex sites are Airport Mesa, Cathedral Rock, Bell Rock, and Boynton Canyon. Some practitioners identify seven or more vortex points in the region. The phenomenon is entirely modern in its New Age framing: in 1980, psychic Page Bryant publicized claims that a spiritual being named Albion told her about the vortexes, an account circulated through an audio recording called 'The Earth Changes Survival Handbook.' This transformed Sedona from a small artist community into a major destination for spiritual tourism. The Yavapai and Apache peoples, whose ancestral lands include the Sedona area, hold traditions of the red rock landscape as sacred territory used for healing ceremonies and vision quests; their traditions predate and are distinct from the New Age vortex framework. Mainstream science does not accept claims of measurable energy vortexes at these sites. The sites' appeal is understood by researchers primarily through the psychological effects of striking geology, the expectation of spiritual experience, and the social context of New Age community.

## Theories & Explanations

### Paranormal / Cryptozoological

**Earth energy vortex** _(Page Bryant, New Age practitioners and tour operators)_

The vortex sites are centers of swirling electromagnetic or spiritual earth energy that can be sensed and used by humans for healing and transformation. Practitioners distinguish between electric (masculine, outward-flowing) and magnetic (feminine, inward-drawing) vortexes.

### Scientific

**Psychological and environmental effect** _(Mainstream psychologists and geologists)_

The experiences reported at vortex sites are explained by the combination of striking natural beauty, expectation effects (visitors come primed to have spiritual experiences), focused meditative attention, altitude and fresh air, and the social reinforcement of a community of seekers. The geology itself — colorful, dramatic, geologically ancient — creates an environment genuinely conducive to reflection.

### Skeptical

**Commercial construction of spiritual significance** _(Skeptical investigators, Cultural economists of spiritual tourism)_

The vortex phenomenon was created and is sustained primarily as a tourism product. Page Bryant's 1980 claims provided the initial narrative framework; the tourism industry of Sedona has invested heavily in perpetuating and elaborating it because of its economic value.


## Indigenous Perspectives

> ⚠️ **The New Age commercialization of Sedona's landscape has been a source of tension with the Yavapai-Apache Nation, whose ancestral territory includes these sites. The vortex framing overlays and sometimes appropriates Native sacred geography without adequate acknowledgment.**

**Cultures**: Yavapai-Apache Nation, Hopi

The Yavapai and Apache peoples have long recognized the Sedona red rock landscape as sacred and have used it for ceremonies, healing practices, and vision quests. This relationship to the land predates and is distinct from the New Age vortex framework.
